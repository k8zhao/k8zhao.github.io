OFFICE HOURS

Office Hours is a platform that facilitates the hosting of virtual office hours and streamlining virtual consultancy at large. As the first and sole product designer for this startup, I built the interface from scratch to create the MVP (minimum viable product). We are in the beginning stages of selling this product to universities.

USER FLOW

Since I was designing this product from scratch, I wanted to sketch out a user flow to understand how users would use the MVP. This user flow walks you through the entry point (homepage/login) of the site to participating in the office hours sessions. Creating this user flow at the start of my process helped me consider what was needed from me in terms of screens to design and visualize the user's experience.

SCREENS FOR MVP

The course dashboard is the first screen users see upon login. It gives them a general overview of all their courses and easy access to live sessions. Courses are split by those in which users are enrolled or those for which they are teaching assistants. Teachers are able to easily create courses, add TA's, and share the course link from this page.

The course library is where all your office hours sessions reside. There is a course library page for each course a user has. Users can search for any video from any course in which they are enrolled. The top search bar allows users to search through their entire course library database, whereas the main search bar searches through videos for that course specifically.

When an office hours session is live, users can either see the session as a student or a TA (depending on their role). Students can submit questions to the queue for TA's to answer and chat other students in the session while listening to TA. Teaching assistants have the ability to select questions in the queue and create sub-sections in the video for easier access later on.

FINAL THOUGHTS

I am not a graphic designer. This was my first time working with a product manager and at the beginning, I felt like I was just there to ‘make things look pretty.’ I did what I was told, but my tasks never made any sense: creating individual screens without understanding its placement in a user flow, adding things just to add things. I was not designing. I soon learned that it’s MY job to ask the right questions: What’s the problem? Who are we trying to solve for? And lay down the foundational research so I can start designing. As a designer, it was my responsibility to take initiative and do what I needed to do to design for their users.
